NFPA 211 Inspection Protocols
🔍 Inspection Levels & Triggers
Level 1 Inspection
Readily accessible areas Routine annual maintenance Same appliance, same fuel
📖 Read the "Why"
The Reality: A Level 1 is a basic visual check. You only perform a Level 1 if the customer has used the system regularly without issue, nothing has changed, and you are just there to sweep. You are only responsible for what you can see with a flashlight.
Level 2 Inspection
Accessible + Camera Scan Sale/Transfer of property Post-chimney fire or weather event Mandatory camera scan
📖 Read the "Why"
The Reality: If a house was just sold, had a chimney fire, or is getting a new fuel type (like a gas log insert), the code mandates a Level 2. You must run a camera up the flue. If you skip the camera, tell them it's fine, and a hidden cracked tile causes a house fire months later, EverSafe is liable. Document the camera scan.
Defects & Flue Maintenance
🚫 Defect Limits & Failure Criteria
Creosote Buildup
Sweeping requirement Level 1/2 Sweep if > 1/8" thick Fails if Stage 3 (Glaze) is present
📖 Read the "Why"
The Reality: A standard brush only removes Stage 1 (sooty) and Stage 2 (crunchy) creosote. Stage 3 looks like shiny, hard, black glass or tar. It is highly concentrated fuel. If it catches fire, it expands up to 7 times its size, blocking the flue and pushing a 2000-degree blowtorch into the living room. Fail it immediately and quote a chemical rotary treatment (PCR).
Flue Liner Integrity
Terra cotta tile defects Zero tolerance for cracks/splits Joint gaps must not exceed 1/8" Fails if ANY tile is cracked
📖 Read the "Why"
The Reality: A terracotta flue tile is designed to contain heat and exhaust. Even a hairline crack or a missing chunk of mortar between tiles breaks that seal. When a fire burns, the draft will pull the heat or deadly carbon monoxide through that crack directly into the home's wood framing or attic. Fail the flue and quote a stainless liner or HeatShield.
Relining & Terminations
🛡️ UL 1777 Stainless Relining
Alloy Selection
Matches fuel type 316Ti / 316L: Wood, Coal, Pellet, Oil AL29-4C: High-Efficiency Gas/Oil ONLY
📖 Read the "Why"
The Reality: Wood fires produce moisture and highly acidic creosote. If you use a cheaper 304 stainless or AL29-4C liner for a wood stove, the acid will literally eat holes through the metal in a few years. 316 Titanium alloy is mandatory for solid fuels because it resists this extreme corrosion.
Liner Insulation
Required for UL listing 1/2" Foil-Faced Wrap (Min) OR Pour-down mix (Thermix)
📖 Read the "Why"
The Reality: Stainless steel conducts heat rapidly. If you drop a bare metal liner into a chimney that is built touching wood framing (zero clearance to combustibles), the heat from the liner will transfer straight through the brick and ignite the wood studs. Wrapping or pouring insulation around the liner stops this heat transfer and keeps the flue warmer, which improves draft.
🏠 Clearances & Terminations
The "3-2-10" Rule
Chimney Termination Height Min 3 ft above roof penetration Min 2 ft higher than structure within 10 ft
📖 Read the "Why"
The Reality: Wind behaves like water; when it hits a roof peak, it splashes and swirls, creating high-pressure zones. If the chimney is too short, that swirling wind will blow straight down the flue, pushing smoke and CO into the living room. Measure 10 feet out: the chimney must be at least 2 feet taller than anything in that circle.
Clearance to Combustibles
Masonry Chimneys Min 2" (Interior Chimneys) Min 1" (Exterior Chimneys)
📖 Read the "Why"
The Reality: This prevents Pyrolysis. When wood framing sits too close to hot masonry for years, the heat slowly bakes the moisture out of the wood, lowering its ignition temperature. Eventually, a normal fire can ignite the wood inside the walls. Drywall, siding, and studs CANNOT directly touch the chimney brick.
Masonry & Hearths
🧱 Masonry, Crowns & Hearths
Chimney Crown (Cast/Poured)
Top seal protection (IRC) Min 2" thickness at thinnest edge Min 2" overhang past brick w/ drip edge Must have bond break around flue
📖 Read the "Why"
The Reality: A proper crown is like an umbrella for the chimney, shedding water away from the bricks. Never pour the concrete tight against the clay flue tile. When the clay gets hot, it expands. If there is no soft expansion joint (bond break) between the clay and the concrete, the expanding flue will crack the crown in half.
Refractory Mortar (ASTM C199)
Fireboxes and clay flues Must use Non-Water Soluble type Max joint thickness: 1/8" to 1/4"
📖 Read the "Why"
The Reality: Standard Portland cement mortar cannot handle extreme temperatures and will quickly crumble inside a firebox. You must use pre-mixed refractory cement (like ChamberTech) designed to withstand 2,000°F without breaking down.
Safety & Weatherproofing
💧 Flashing & Fall Protection
Cricket / Saddle Rules
Diverting water behind chimney Required if chimney is > 30" wide
📖 Read the "Why"
The Reality: If the back of the chimney facing the roof peak is wider than 30 inches, it acts like a giant dam. Leaves, snow, and rain will pile up behind it, eventually forcing water under the shingles. You must build a small peaked roof (cricket) behind the chimney to split the water and shed it down the sides.
Fall Protection (OSHA)
When harnesses are required Any work at or above 6 feet
📖 Read the "Why"
The Reality: OSHA regulations are written in blood. If your boots are 6 feet or higher off the lower level, you are required to wear a harness tied off to a secure roof anchor (Personal Fall Arrest System), or have guardrails in place. A fall from 10 feet is frequently fatal.
