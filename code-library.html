🕵️ Chimney Fire Forensics
NFPA 211 - 14.5
Visual Evidence of Hostile Fire Puffed, honeycomb-like creosote Vertical "clean" cracks in clay tiles Warped damper plates / discolored caps
📖 Read the "Why"

The Reality: Most chimney fires go unnoticed by the homeowner because they sound like a low rumble or a strong wind. The extreme, sudden heat (2000°F+) instantly turns flat creosote into expanded, puffy honeycomb. It also causes the clay tiles to expand faster than they can handle, creating massive, vertical, razor-thin cracks (Thermal Shock).

📸 REQUIRED PHOTOS: 1x Puffed creosote texture, 1x Vertical tile crack, 1x Warped metal or heat discoloration.

NFPA 211 - 14.9

🌬️ Draft & Negative Pressure
IRC R1006.1
Combustion Air Requirements Required for solid fuel appliances in tight homes Smoke spillage upon ignition
📖 Read the "Why"

The Physics: Fire needs oxygen. A standard fireplace pulls 300+ cubic feet of air per minute out of the house. If the house is sealed tight with modern windows, or if a bathroom fan/kitchen hood is running, a vacuum forms. The chimney is the largest hole in the house, so the house literally sucks air *down* the chimney, bringing smoke into the living room.

The Test: Crack a window near the fireplace 2 inches. If the smoke immediately reverses and drafts perfectly up the chimney, the house is negatively pressurized. You must install a dedicated outside air kit.

🦝 Wildlife & Biohazards
Federal MBTA Law
Chimney Swifts (Migratory Birds) Federally protected species DO NOT REMOVE active nests or eggs
📖 Read the "Why"

The Law: Chimney Swifts are protected under the Federal Migratory Bird Treaty Act. If you knowingly knock down a nest with eggs or chicks, you and the company can be fined up to $15,000 and face jail time. You must wait for them to fly south in the fall, then sweep the flue and install a cap.
OSHA Biohazard PPE
Animal Droppings & Histoplasmosis Raccoon, Bat, and Bird Guano Mandatory P100 / N95 Respirator
📖 Read the "Why"

The Danger: Droppings (especially bird and bat) contain spores of a fungus called Histoplasma. When you sweep, these invisible spores go airborne. If inhaled, they cause Histoplasmosis, a severe and potentially fatal lung infection. Never sweep heavy guano without a high-grade respirator and HEPA vacuum control.
