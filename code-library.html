NFPA 211 Inspection Protocols
🔍 Inspection Levels & Triggers
Level 1 Inspection
Readily accessible areas Routine annual maintenance Same appliance, same fuel
📖 Read the "Why"

The Reality: A Level 1 is a basic visual check of the portions of the chimney you can see without moving anything. "Readily accessible" means no tools required.

The Limit: You only perform a Level 1 if the customer has used the system regularly without issue, nothing has changed, and you are just there to sweep. If you have to take a stove pipe apart, you've moved past a Level 1.
Level 2 Inspection
Accessible + Camera Scan Sale/Transfer of property Post-chimney fire or weather event Mandatory camera scan
📖 Read the "Why"

The Reality: "Accessible" means you can use hand tools to open doors, remove panels, or detach a stove pipe to see inside. You do not break drywall or brick.

The Liability: If a house was just sold, had a chimney fire, or is getting a new fuel type, the code mandates a Level 2. You must run a camera up the flue. If you skip the camera, mark it "good," and a hidden cracked tile causes a house fire months later, EverSafe is liable. Document the scan.
Level 3 Inspection
Concealed Areas Suspected concealed hazard Requires destructive access
📖 Read the "Why"

The Reality: This is demolition. If you did a Level 2 and suspect there is a massive framing clearance violation or a broken thimble hidden behind the drywall or masonry, you recommend a Level 3.

The Execution: We literally cut holes in the walls or tear down brick to see what is happening. We never quote this lightly, but we never ignore a hidden hazard.
Defects & Flue Maintenance
🚫 Defect Limits & Failure Criteria
Creosote Buildup
Sweeping requirement Sweep if > 1/8" thick Fails if Stage 3 (Glaze) is present
📖 Read the "Why"

The Physics: Creosote forms when flue gases cool down too quickly and condense on the walls of the chimney. This happens from burning wet, unseasoned wood or having a flue that is too large/cold (poor draft).

The Danger: A standard brush only removes Stage 1 (sooty) and Stage 2 (crunchy). Stage 3 looks like shiny, hard, black glass. It is highly concentrated fuel. If it catches fire, it expands up to 7 times its size, blocking the flue and pushing a 2000-degree blowtorch into the living room. Fail it immediately and quote a chemical rotary treatment (PCR).
Flue Liner Integrity
Terra cotta tile defects Zero tolerance for cracks/splits Fails if ANY tile is cracked
📖 Read the "Why"

The Physics (Thermal Shock): Clay tiles fail due to thermal shock—heating up and cooling down too rapidly, usually from a chimney fire or sudden water intrusion on a hot flue. They don't just "crack from old age."

The Danger: A terracotta flue tile is designed to contain heat and exhaust. Even a hairline crack breaks that seal. When a fire burns, the draft will pull the heat or deadly carbon monoxide through that crack directly into the home's wood framing. There is no such thing as an "okay" crack. Fail the flue and quote a stainless liner or HeatShield.
Smoke Chamber Parging
Transition zone below the flue Must be parged smooth Fails if brick steps are exposed
📖 Read the "Why"

The Physics: The smoke chamber is an inverted funnel above the damper. If the brickwork is exposed and stepped (corbeled), the smoke hits those jagged edges and rolls backward. This turbulence slows the draft down to a crawl, causing smoke to spill into the house and massive creosote buildup.

The Fix: Parging fills those steps with refractory mortar, creating a perfectly smooth, aerodynamic funnel so the smoke rockets straight up the flue. Fail exposed corbeling and quote a parging repair (ChamberTech).
Relining & Terminations
🛡️ UL 1777 Stainless Relining
Alloy Selection
Matches fuel type 316Ti / 316L: Wood, Coal, Pellet, Oil AL29-4C: High-Efficiency Gas ONLY
📖 Read the "Why"

Wood (316Ti): Wood fires get incredibly hot and produce highly acidic creosote. If you use a cheaper 304 or AL29-4C liner for a wood stove, the acid and heat will literally eat holes through the metal in a few years. 316 Titanium alloy is mandatory for solid fuels because it resists extreme heat and corrosion.

Gas (AL29-4C): High-efficiency gas furnaces barely vent any heat, meaning the exhaust is mostly warm, highly acidic water vapor. AL29-4C is specifically engineered to handle massive amounts of corrosive condensation without rusting out.
Liner Insulation
Required for UL listing 1/2" Foil-Faced Wrap (Min) OR Pour-down mix (Thermix)
📖 Read the "Why"

Protection: Stainless steel conducts heat rapidly. If you drop a bare metal liner into a chimney that touches wood framing (zero clearance to combustibles), the heat transfers straight through the brick and ignites the wood studs. Wrapping it stops the transfer.

Performance: Heat rises. If the liner isn't insulated, the surrounding cold masonry sucks the heat out of the exhaust gas. The smoke slows down, draft fails, and creosote dumps rapidly. Insulation keeps the inner liner hot, ensuring a fast, clean draft.
🏠 Clearances & Terminations
The "3-2-10" Rule
Chimney Termination Height Min 3 ft above roof penetration Min 2 ft higher than structure within 10 ft
📖 Read the "Why"

The Physics: Wind behaves like water; when it hits a roof peak, it splashes and swirls, creating high-pressure zones. If the chimney is too short, that swirling high-pressure wind will cap the chimney and blow straight down the flue, pushing smoke and CO directly into the living room.

The Execution: Measure 10 feet horizontally outward from the chimney top. The chimney must be built at least 2 feet taller than anything inside that 10-foot circle (like the roof peak, a dormer, or a wall) to keep the exhaust out of the wind turbulence.
Clearance to Combustibles
Masonry Chimneys Min 2" (Interior Chimneys) Min 1" (Exterior Chimneys)
📖 Read the "Why"

The Physics (Pyrolysis): When wood framing sits too close to hot masonry for years, the heat slowly bakes the moisture out of the wood, permanently altering its chemical structure. Its ignition temperature drops drastically. Eventually, a totally normal fire in the fireplace can ignite the wood inside the walls. Drywall, siding, and studs CANNOT directly touch the chimney brick.

The Field Reality: Houses settle. Foundations sink. If the chimney is built tight to the framing, the settling house will actually hang off the chimney, cracking the masonry. The air gap allows for natural expansion and settling.
Masonry & Hearths
🧱 Masonry, Crowns & Hearths
Chimney Crown (Cast/Poured)
Top seal protection (IRC) Min 2" overhang past brick w/ drip edge Must have bond break around flue
📖 Read the "Why"

The Bond Break: Never pour concrete tight against the clay flue. When the clay gets hot, it expands outward. If there is no soft expansion joint (bond break) between the clay and the concrete, the expanding flue will literally rip the concrete crown apart.

The Drip Edge: Water is sticky (capillary action). If a crown is flush with the bricks, rain will run down the concrete, curl under, and soak the brick face, causing spalling (flaking). The crown must overhang by 2", and have a groove (drip edge) cut underneath it to force the water to drip off into the air, completely missing the bricks.
ASTM Mortar Types
Compressive Strength Type N: Standard brickwork (750 psi) Type S: Structural/Below Grade (1800 psi)
📖 Read the "Why"

The Physics: You do NOT want the strongest mortar possible for a chimney. A chimney is constantly swaying in the wind and expanding with heat. Type N is softer and more flexible. If you use ultra-hard Type S mortar on a standard chimney repair, the rigid mortar will refuse to flex, and the bricks themselves will crack under the pressure. The mortar is supposed to be the sacrificial element.
Safety & Weatherproofing
💧 Flashing & Fall Protection
Step & Counter Flashing
Waterproofing the roof seam Counter flashing cut 1" into mortar Fails if only glued/caulked to brick face
📖 Read the "Why"

The Physics: The roof framing and the masonry chimney expand, contract, and settle at totally different rates. You MUST use a two-part system. The "Step" flashing is nailed to the roof deck. The "Counter" flashing is ground into the masonry joint and hangs down over the step flashing like a skirt.

The Failure: If you nail a single piece of metal to the roof AND glue it to the brick, the differing movement of the house vs. the chimney will tear the metal or caulk open within a single season, causing massive water leaks.
Cricket / Saddle
Diverting water behind chimney Required if chimney is > 30" wide
📖 Read the "Why"

The Physics: If the back of the chimney facing the roof peak is wider than 30 inches, it acts like a giant dam. Leaves, snow, and rain will pile up behind it. The standing water builds hydrostatic pressure, which will eventually force water back up under the shingles. You must build a small peaked roof (cricket) behind the chimney to split the water and shed it down the sides.
