🔍 Inspection Levels & Triggers
NFPA 211 - 15.3.1
Level 1 Inspection
Readily accessible areas Routine annual maintenance Same appliance, same fuel
📖 Read the "Why"

The Reality: A Level 1 is a basic visual check of the portions of the chimney you can see without moving anything. "Readily accessible" means no tools required.
NFPA 211 - 15.4.1
Level 2 Inspection
Accessible + Camera Scan Sale/Transfer of property Post-chimney fire or weather event Mandatory camera scan
📖 Read the "Why"

The Liability: If a house was just sold, had a chimney fire, or is getting a new fuel type, the code mandates a Level 2. You must run a camera up the flue. Document the scan to protect EverSafe's liability.
NFPA 211 - 15.5.1
Level 3 Inspection
Concealed Areas Suspected concealed hazard Requires destructive access
📖 Read the "Why"

The Reality: This is demolition. If you did a Level 2 and suspect there is a massive framing clearance violation hidden behind the drywall or masonry, you recommend a Level 3.
🚫 Defect Limits & Failure Criteria
NFPA 211 - 14.4.1
Creosote Buildup Sweep if > 1/8" thick Fails if Stage 3 (Glaze) is present
📖 Read the "Why"

The Danger: Stage 3 looks like shiny, hard, black glass. It expands up to 7 times its size when ignited, blocking the flue and pushing a 2000-degree blowtorch into the living room. Fail it immediately and quote a chemical rotary treatment (PCR).
NFPA 211 - 14.9
Flue Liner Integrity Zero tolerance for cracks/splits Fails if ANY tile is cracked
📖 Read the "Why"

The Danger: Clay tiles fail due to thermal shock. Even a hairline crack breaks the seal, allowing heat and carbon monoxide to escape into the home's wood framing. Fail the flue and quote a stainless liner or HeatShield.
IRC R1003.8
Smoke Chamber Parging Must be parged smooth Fails if brick steps are exposed
📖 Read the "Why"

The Physics: If the brickwork is exposed and stepped, the smoke hits those jagged edges and rolls backward. This turbulence slows the draft down, causing smoke to spill into the house. Parging fills those steps with refractory mortar.
☔ Waterproofing & Preservation
ASTM E514
Masonry Water Repellents Must be 100% vapor permeable Fails if painted or film-sealed
📖 Read the "Why"

The Physics: Bricks are like hard sponges; they absorb water and need to breathe it out. If you coat a chimney with paint, Thompsons Water Seal, or acrylics, it traps the moisture inside the brick. When winter hits, that trapped water freezes, expands, and blows the face off the brick (spalling).
ASTM C920
Joint & Flashing Sealants Elastomeric Polyurethane or Silicone Fails if sealed with roofing tar/mastic
📖 Read the "Why"

The Physics: Chimneys get incredibly hot, and roofs get baked by the sun. Cheap acrylic caulk or black roofing tar dries out, cracks, and pulls away from the masonry within a year. You must use high-flex, UV-resistant elastomeric sealants that can stretch with the house.
Crown Repair
Elastomeric Crown Coatings Used for cracks < 1/8" wide Rebuild required if structurally compromised
📖 Read the "Why"

The Rule: Brushable crown sealants (like CrownCoat) are fantastic for sealing shrinking cracks and preserving a crown. However, they are not structural. If the concrete is loose, crumbling, or the cracks are wider than 1/8", you cannot just paint over it. It must be torn off and re-poured.

🔥 Gas Logs & Dampers (IFGC)
IFGC 602.1
Top-Mount Dampers Prohibited on active Gas Logs
📖 Read the "Why"

The Fatality Risk: If a top-mount damper breaks closed over a Gas Log set, there is no smoke. The room fills with invisible, odorless Carbon Monoxide. Never install a top-sealer on a gas appliance.
IFGC 602.2
Damper Clamps (Vented Gas) Mandatory for Vented Gas Logs Must lock damper open permanently
IFGC 603.1
Unvented (Vent-Free) Gas Logs Requires exact room volume sizing Usually prohibited in bedrooms/baths
🏭 Prefab Systems & Ash Pits
UL 127
Factory-Built Fireplaces (Zero-Clearance) Must use OEM replacement parts Fails if parts are mixed & matched
📖 Read the "Why"

The Liability: A prefab fireplace is tested in a lab as a complete assembly. If you connect Brand X pipe to a Brand Y firebox, you instantly void the UL listing. Always order OEM parts.
IRC R1003.5
Ash Pits & Cleanout Doors Must be cast iron or tight steel Min 6" clearance to combustibles

🏠 Clearances & Terminations
IRC R1003.9
The "3-2-10" Rule Min 3 ft above roof penetration Min 2 ft higher than structure within 10 ft
📖 View Diagram & Explanation

Measure 10 feet out. The chimney must be built at least 2 feet taller than anything in that circle to prevent wind downdrafts from pushing CO into the home.
IRC R1003.18
Clearance to Combustibles Min 2" (Interior Chimneys) Min 1" (Exterior Chimneys)
🧱 Masonry, Crowns & Hearths
IRC R1003.9.1
Chimney Crown (Cast/Poured) Min 2" overhang past brick w/ drip edge Must have bond break around flue
📖 Read the "Why"

The Bond Break: Never pour concrete tight against the clay flue. When the clay gets hot, it expands. If there is no soft expansion joint (bond break), the expanding flue will literally rip the concrete crown apart.
ASTM C270
ASTM Mortar Types Type N: Standard brickwork (750 psi) Type S: Structural/Below Grade (1800 psi)
💧 Flashing & Fall Protection
IRC R1003.20
Step & Counter Flashing Counter flashing cut 1" into mortar Fails if only glued/caulked to brick face
📖 Read the "Why"

The Physics: The roof framing and the chimney settle at totally different rates. You MUST use a two-part system. If you nail a single piece of metal to the roof AND glue it to the brick, the differing movement will tear the caulk open in a single season.
IRC R1003.20
Cricket / Saddle Required if chimney is > 30" wide
OSHA 1926.501
Fall Protection Any work at or above 6 feet
